# Answer Quality Guide: The Specificity Ladder

**Created:** 2026-02-14
**Last Updated:** 2026-02-14
**Purpose:** Teaching guide for giving answers that produce sharp specs
**Status:** Final

## The Problem: Vague Answers Produce Generic Content

Every answer you give during the SDD workflow shapes the content that gets produced. Vague answers lead to vague specs, which lead to generic content that serves no one well. Specific answers lead to sharp specs, which lead to targeted content that resonates with your actual audience.

This guide teaches you how to give answers at the level of specificity that produces excellent results.

---

## The Specificity Ladder

### Level 1: Vague (Danger Zone)

**Example:** "The audience is mid-career professionals."

What's wrong: Who are they? What do they do all day? What frustrates them? What have they tried? This description fits millions of people, so content written for this audience will be generic enough to bore all of them.

**Result:** Generic module length. Generic examples. Generic tone. Content that tries to serve everyone and succeeds with no one.

---

### Level 2: Somewhat Specific (Getting Closer)

**Example:** "The audience is mid-career software engineers interested in AI."

Better: We know the field and the interest. But "interested in AI" could mean anything — hobby interest, career pivot, team assignment, curiosity.

**Result:** Modules that are directionally right but miss the mark on depth, examples, and emotional connection.

---

### Level 3: Specific (Good — This Is the Minimum Target)

**Example:** "Software engineers with 5-8 years experience who've built production features but never used AI in their work, and are now frustrated because their team is adopting AI tools and they don't know where to start."

Now we know: their experience level (skilled, not beginners), their specific frustration (team is moving and they feel behind), their context (production engineers, not hobbyists), and what they've tried (nothing yet — they need a starting point).

**Result:** Modules calibrated to their skill level. Examples drawn from production engineering. Tone that respects their experience while acknowledging the learning curve. Exercises that bridge from what they know to what they need.

---

### Level 4: Extremely Specific (Best — For Critical Decisions)

**Example:** "Sarah is a 6-year backend engineer at a Series B startup. She's great at database design and scaling systems. The company just decided to add AI features and assigned her to lead the effort. She knows Python well but has never used LLMs, doesn't understand how to structure prompts, and is worried about API costs and response latency. She learns best from real code examples and case studies, not theory. She has about 90 minutes of focused learning time in the evenings after her kids go to bed."

Now we know everything: her name (makes her real in our minds), her strengths (database, scaling — we can use analogies from these), her specific gap (LLMs, prompts, costs), her constraints (evening learner, 90-minute windows), and her learning style (code examples, not theory).

**Result:** 90-minute modules. Python code examples using database analogies. Cost-conscious implementation guidance. Practical, no-theory tone. Exercises that produce working code she can bring to work the next day.

---

## How Specificity Changes Everything

| Decision | Level 1 Answer | Level 4 Answer |
|----------|---------------|----------------|
| Module length | "Reasonable" | "90 minutes — matches evening study windows" |
| Examples | "Relevant code samples" | "Python FastAPI endpoints with LLM calls, using database design analogies she'll recognize" |
| Tone | "Professional" | "Direct, respects her engineering experience, never condescending about AI basics" |
| Exercises | "Practice problems" | "Working code she deploys to a test endpoint and measures cost/latency" |
| Pricing | "Competitive" | "$497 — she'd expense it if she could, pay personally if not, but $1000+ is a conversation with her partner" |

The Level 4 spec produces a course Sarah would love. The Level 1 spec produces a course nobody feels strongly about.

## When You Don't Know: The [UNSURE] Principle

Sometimes you can't be specific because you genuinely don't know. That is completely fine — and it's actually one of the most valuable things you can communicate.

**The principle:** [UNSURE] is always better than a confident wrong answer.

Here's why: A confident wrong answer gets baked into the spec and produces wrong content across every module, exercise, and example. An [UNSURE] flag gets resolved with evidence and produces right content.

### What [UNSURE] Looks Like in Practice

**Question:** "Should this course be self-paced or cohort-based?"

**Bad answer (guessing):** "Self-paced." *(You picked this because it sounds easier, not because you have evidence.)*

**Good answer (flagging uncertainty):**
"[UNSURE] — I'm not sure. I think self-paced makes sense because our audience has irregular schedules, but I've never run a cohort-based course so I don't know what I'm missing. Can we research this?"

**What happens next:**
1. AI runs a 10-15 minute research session on self-paced vs. cohort models for your audience type
2. Returns with evidence-based options
3. You decide with information instead of guessing

**The result:** A decision grounded in evidence that you can explain and defend, instead of a guess that might need to be reversed later.

### [UNSURE] Is Not Failure

Think of [UNSURE] the way an engineer thinks of a test case. A test that reveals a bug is not a failure — it's doing its job. Similarly, flagging uncertainty reveals a decision that needs more information. The SDD workflow is designed to handle this gracefully.

What the workflow CANNOT handle gracefully is false certainty — answers that sound confident but are wrong.

## Practice: Climbing the Ladder

Try these exercises to build your specificity muscle.

### Exercise 1: Audience Description

Start with: "Business professionals"

Push to Level 3: *Who specifically? What's their role? How many years in? What's their specific frustration? What have they tried? What didn't work?*

Your answer: _______________

### Exercise 2: Product Description

Start with: "An online course"

Push to Level 3: *About what specifically? How long? What format? What does the learner walk away with? How is this different from the 50 other courses on this topic?*

Your answer: _______________

### Exercise 3: Pricing Decision

Start with: "Competitive pricing"

Push to Level 3: *What number? Why that number? What would your specific audience think when they see that price? Would they pay it without thinking, or would it be a decision? Who in their life would they discuss it with?*

Your answer: _______________

### Exercise 4: Success Metric

Start with: "Good engagement"

Push to Level 3: *What number? Completion rate? Time-on-page? Exercise completion? Net promoter score? What specific behavior tells you this product worked?*

Your answer: _______________

## Quick Reference: Before You Answer Any SDD Question

1. **Restate the product.** "We're building [IDENTITY STATEMENT]." If that sentence feels off, fix the identity before answering.

2. **Picture your specific user.** Not "professionals" — the actual person. What are they doing right now? What frustrates them? What would make them say "finally, someone gets it"?

3. **Answer for THIS product, not your business in general.** The question isn't "Who do you serve?" — it's "Who is this specific product for, and what moment in their life does it serve?"

4. **Include your reasoning.** Not just "Option B" but "Option B because our audience has 90-minute evening windows and deeper modules respect that constraint."

5. **Flag what you don't know.** [UNSURE] with a clear explanation of what's unclear is worth more than a confident guess.

---

**Remember:** You don't need to be at Level 4 for every answer. Level 3 is the target. But for the most important decisions — audience, pricing, product identity — push for Level 4. Those decisions cascade through everything else.
